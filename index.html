 Education Technology 

for the 21st Century

Bronx.Tech Case Study for three month design process in 2018

Overview

I was introduced to the two founders at Bronx.Tech in Spring of 2018. They had proposed an idea for an education application that would begin on the desktop, then eventually make its way to being a mobile application. Unfortunately, neither had any technical background, so it was an idea that was not quite drawn out, literally and figuratively. Their problem was straightforward. They wanted to figure out a way to connect educators that spend their days in the classroom with professionals from technology companies across the city. More simply: 

How can we create a way for teachers to connect with companies for expertise purposes or for recommending students to possible positions?

The initial idea for a platform would be a forum separated into two factions - teachers and professions. The teachers would be from high schools that signed onto the platform and would be able to ask questions regarding certain topics that may relate to a professional's industry. We began with computer science, since that's where the majority of jobs circulated. Once the teacher asked, a professional from a software company, for example, would be able to give a qualitative answer. To incentive the professionals, students would be able to find internships or jobs after graduating from the platform. While students wouldn't directly be able to access the site, teachers would act as their liaisons.

The idea eventually expanded to include more features, including a meet-and-greet calendar and a gamification component that would rank users as novices to experts. This case study will explain the process taken to get this idea to a desktop. 


My Role

The project began as an idea without any proper wireframes. The two cofounders had business backgrounds, so they were able to get audiences with schools and companies, but without anything tangible to present. I came in to consider all the proposed ideas, turn it into a design, and eventually convert this project into an MVP using React.js. 


The Users

The users would be split into two different groups. 

First, teachers would be able to use the platform to ask questions that can be tagged with specific topics. They would want to use the platform to represent their schools and possibly land their students great jobs.

Second, professions would be able to use the platform to connect with teachers. They would be incentivized by being able to gain employment from schools across the city (There may also the tax benefits in working with schools).

There isn't any real difference between the groups other than their signup email (a school vs a company). Each has potential to gain clout on the platform by participation in the forum. Better answers/interactions have a better chance at becoming experts in particular fields. 

The Process

Creating an MVP meant making a forum that can be used by both teachers and professionals. I approached this by looking at some of the most popular forums out there, including Reddit and StackOverflow. Both have the same vertical structure that relied mainly on a comments and upvotes. In terms of the platform we wanted to build, the upvoting system made sense for a sort of gamification element - if we wanted a teacher or professional to be taken seriously on a particular topic, they would be incentivized to create good content, namely posts that taught something.

Reddit, StackOverflow, and other forum sites had similar structures, so making a sort of forum would need to resemble this familiar setup. I sketched out some initial ideas and eventually converted these into some wireframes. 

I would eventually add a spot for tags in the final version and switch the spot of the user.

Ah, the user. The ultra-important piece of this application. He or she would need prominence, so a profile page was necessary. With the logo and branding already resembling Facebook slightly with the blue color, it was my goal to keep this looking as different as possible, but familiar enough that users would understand everything.

Original concepts had a calendar aspect. The user would be able to schedule time on another user's calendar. This idea made sense, but this, in addition to a messaging element, risked taking the user off the platform. With hardly a userbase to begin with, it was important not to lose connections to other sources like Facebook or Google Calendar, so these ideas were eventually scrapped.

Then there was the gamification element. This idea, originally considered in a brainstorming with the founders, took on a life of its own and really gave the application its own feel. Facebook and other applications were great for things like messaging - this application may not be the place for that. Instead, I needed to focus on getting the gamification element to make sense on a user's page. Inspiration came from my childhood of course. Let's take it back to the 90's.


The idea spread into wireframes and resembled what looked like those visualized bars as strengths of particular users. If a user answered a lot of questions that were tagged in a particular topic, and other people upvoted this, this user would gain clout on the platform and could eventually reach expert level.

With a few wireframes in place, research was done by asking potential users what they would like to see out of this platform. There was fear of using 'yet another social media platform,' yet this one seemed extra annoying, since it was for work and not pleasure. It would need to be fun, so calculating where the fun was (hint hint - gamification) was all the importance.

The most popular feature was the point system, which at this early stage, was still being discovered. Some popular questions included:

  • How exactly would points be awarded?
  • How would we block friends from just voting up each other's scores?
  • Can any tags be created? Wouldn't these need to be limited?

These questions and others would need to be figured out eventually. In the meantime, some awesome designs were created for the initial presentation of the page to investors.

The Takeaway 

The design process started from zero, with the exception of a created logo and color scheme, so I had to do plenty of research into similar kinds of sites, like Reddit and Facebook, to see what was prioritized to keep users interested.

These designs helped push the product in meetings with investors from schools and companies alike. For the development aspect, we went with React for the front-end, since I had experience using it, and Meteor on the backend, since a Backend Developer came on to help. The product signed on to over a couple dozen schools and companies.

Bronx.Tech became a great tool to connect two different types of workers that normally wouldn't share the same water cooler. It solved a problem that wasn't initially considered. With technology as simple as a web forum, we were able to onboard a pretty great userbase.
